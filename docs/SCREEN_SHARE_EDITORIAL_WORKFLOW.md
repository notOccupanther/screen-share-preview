# Screen Share — Editorial Agent Workflow

## Purpose
Screen Share is intended to operate like a small editorial newsroom rather than a single monolithic prompt. The goal is to use specialised agents for distinct sections, then bring everything through a unified editorial layer before anything is updated on the site.

This is a drafting and curation workflow, not an autonomous publishing workflow.

## Core Principle
No section agent publishes directly.

Agents research, draft, and propose.
The Lead Editor assembles and normalises.
Niall gives final approval.
Only then does packaging/deployment update the site.

---

## Niall's Role
Niall is not just the approver at the end.

Niall works with the Lead Editor to shape:
- the weekly theme
- the editorial posture of the issue
- what the site should lean into or avoid that week
- how much weight should go to criticism, discovery, industry conscience, or curation
- what should count as signal versus noise

In effect, Niall acts as editor-in-chief and strategic taste anchor.

---

## Lead Editor Agent
### Role
The Lead Editor is the coordinating editorial brain for Screen Share.

### Responsibilities
- Work with Niall to define the issue's theme, energy, and purpose
- Choose or validate the lead film and overall editorial spine
- Brief the section agents
- Review all section outputs for coherence, tone, repetition, and strength
- Decide where sections need revision before approval
- Assemble a final issue draft for review

### Output
- Issue brief
- Section assignments / prompts
- Coherence pass across all sections
- Final editorial assembly for Niall's approval

### Important note
The Lead Editor does not replace Niall.
The Lead Editor is a collaborator that helps shape both the inputs and the final output of the site.

## Section Agents

### 1. The Rewind Agent
**Purpose:** Select and frame the archive film that speaks to the present moment.

**Responsibilities:**
- Find the archive title
- Explain why it matters now
- Draft contextual editorial framing
- Avoid nostalgia for its own sake

**Output:**
- Film choice
- Why-now rationale
- Rewind copy
- Optional visual/reference suggestions

---

### 2. The Argument Agent
**Purpose:** Produce the week's central question or tension.

**Responsibilities:**
- Identify one worthwhile interpretive disagreement
- Draft competing readings clearly
- Keep the tension intelligent, not clickbaity

**Output:**
- Core question
- Two or more readings
- Framing copy

---

### 3. Mood-First Discovery Agent
**Purpose:** Curate films by emotional state rather than genre.

**Responsibilities:**
- Define useful mood buckets
- Select films that genuinely fit them
- Write short human tags and rationale
- Avoid stale recommendation-engine behaviour

**Output:**
- Mood buckets
- Film selections
- Tags / micro-copy

---

### 4. Weekly Pulse Agent
**Purpose:** Capture what the film conversation looked like that week.

**Responsibilities:**
- Surface what is being discussed most
- Identify the most divisive title(s)
- Spot hidden gems or resurgent films
- Summarise the pattern of conversation, not just raw noise

**Output:**
- Pulse cards / structured data
- Short editorial notes
- Confidence notes where signal is thin

---

### 5. Behind the Frame Agent
**Purpose:** Focus on craft and form.

**Responsibilities:**
- Choose a formal or technical aspect worth spotlighting
- Explain how craft creates feeling
- Write for intelligent readers without becoming dry or academic sludge

**Output:**
- Craft angle
- Short essay / note
- Optional still or sequence recommendation

---

### 6. Behind the Curtain Agent
**Purpose:** Cover industry and media stories with conscience.

**Responsibilities:**
- Track stories with real cultural, political, or institutional weight
- Ignore routine casting churn and filler trade news
- Explain why a story matters beneath the headline

**Output:**
- 2–3 selected stories
- Framing note for each
- Source list
- Significance summary

---

### 7. Visual Direction Agent
**Purpose:** Help the issue feel visually intentional.

**Responsibilities:**
- Recommend the hero treatment
- Shape the visual mood of the issue
- Ensure the motion layer remains subtle and cinematic
- Prevent the site from feeling visually accidental or over-decorated

**Output:**
- Hero art-direction note
- Section emphasis guidance
- Asset / placeholder recommendations

---

### 8. Packaging Agent
**Purpose:** Turn approved editorial material into site-ready structure.

**Responsibilities:**
- Convert approved content into HTML/JSON-ready form
- Build preview output
- Validate links, ordering, and structure
- Prepare live update package after approval

**Output:**
- Site-ready content package
- Preview build
- Deploy-ready update

### Important note
The Packaging Agent should never make editorial decisions on its own.

## Flipboard as a Formal Input Stream
Niall's regular Flipboard curation for TV and movies should be treated as a primary editorial input.

This matters because it reflects:
- current taste and attention
- themes already emerging in ongoing curation
- stories, films, and conversations Niall is already selecting for signal

### How Flipboard should feed the workflow
The Lead Editor should review Flipboard curation at the start of each issue cycle and use it as:
- a source of candidate lead films or themes
- a source of discussion threads worth developing
- a signal for what belongs in Weekly Pulse or Behind the Curtain
- a check against drift, so the site stays connected to Niall's real curation practice

### Best use of Flipboard
Not as raw copy-paste source material.
Instead, as:
- editorial signal
- source discovery input
- taste calibration
- discussion prompt material

## Weekly Workflow

### Stage 1 — Editorial Brief
Niall + Lead Editor define:
- the week's theme
- the lead film
- the energy of the issue
- any must-include stories or angles
- whether Flipboard curation suggests a strong direction

### Stage 2 — Section Drafting
Section agents draft in parallel:
- Rewind
- Argument
- Mood Discovery
- Weekly Pulse
- Behind the Frame
- Behind the Curtain

### Stage 3 — Editorial Synthesis
Lead Editor reviews all outputs and asks:
- Does this feel like one issue?
- Is there repetition or drift?
- Is any section weak enough to require rewrite?
- Does the issue reflect both Screen Share's identity and Niall's judgment?

### Stage 4 — Preview Packaging
Packaging Agent assembles:
- preview page
- section pages
- structured content/data
- reviewable issue package

### Stage 5 — Niall Approval
Niall reviews for:
- quality
- coherence
- tone
- usefulness
- readiness to publish

### Stage 6 — Publish
Only approved content is pushed live.

---

## Output Contract for All Agents
Every agent should report back in a consistent shape:

- Summary
- Proposed section output
- Why it belongs in this issue
- Sources / evidence
- Risks / uncertainties
- Recommendation: ready for approval or needs revision

This makes review faster and keeps the workflow legible.

---

## Editorial Guardrails
- No autonomous publishing
- No filler sections just to complete a template
- No recommendation-engine tone
- No empty entertainment-news churn
- Keep motion subtle and cinematic
- Keep the site feeling composed, not procedurally generated
- Let Flipboard inform editorial direction, but do not let it flatten the publication into link aggregation

---

## Recommended First Implementation
Start lean.

### Phase 1 agents
- Lead Editor
- Rewind
- Argument
- Weekly Pulse
- Behind the Curtain
- Packaging

### Phase 2 agents
- Mood-First Discovery
- Behind the Frame
- Visual Direction

This keeps the core editorial identity strong before expanding the workflow.

---

## In One Line
Screen Share should run like a curated editorial studio: specialised agents draft sections, the Lead Editor works with Niall to shape the issue, Flipboard feeds the weekly signal, and nothing goes live without explicit approval.
