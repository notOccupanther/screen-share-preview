# Screen Share — Future Platform Direction

## Note
Screen Share is not only about films.

It should be understood as covering:
- movies
- TV shows
- the broader culture of watching
- the conversations, arguments, and patterns that emerge around screen-based storytelling

That distinction matters for future product design.

---

## The Thought
Once Screen Share is strong as a product in its own right — editorially, visually, technically, and operationally — it may be worth exploring whether it could evolve into a platform that creates personalised Screen Share editions for individual users.

In that version, a user could potentially connect some combination of:
- Letterboxd
- Trakt
- Netflix / viewing-history sources where feasible
- watchlists
- ratings
- saved lists
- other media-viewing signals

The result would not simply be recommendations.

The result would be a personalised Screen Share edition: an editorialised weekly or periodic publication shaped around that person's actual viewing life.

---

## Why This Is Interesting
Most existing products in this space do one of four things:
- log what you watched
- recommend what to watch next
- aggregate ratings and reviews
- provide generic one-to-many editorial coverage

A personalised Screen Share platform would aim at something more distinctive:

### One-to-one editorial interpretation
Not just:
- here is what people are watching
- here is what is trending
- here is a recommendation list

But:
- here is what your viewing week meant
- here is the argument you are most likely to care about
- here is the archive title that speaks to your current taste pattern
- here is what your recent movie and TV viewing suggests about your habits, moods, curiosities, and blind spots

That is a more interesting product category than simple recommendation software.

---

## What a Personal Edition Could Include
A future personalised Screen Share edition might include:

- **Hero** — the film or TV title most worth foregrounding from a user's recent orbit
- **The Rewind** — an older film or series that rhymes with their current taste and moment
- **The Argument** — a debate or interpretive question drawn from something they have watched
- **Mood-First Discovery** — suggestions shaped by their emotional viewing patterns, not just genre history
- **Weekly Pulse** — what conversations matter around the titles they care about
- **Behind the Frame** — a craft or form note tied to something they watched recently
- **Behind the Curtain** — industry/media context relevant to their interests
- **Taste drift / blind spots** — what they are circling, avoiding, over-indexing on, or missing

This would effectively become a weekly magazine about the user's own screen life.

---

## Why This Should Not Be the Immediate Product
This is promising, but it should not distract from the core mission too early.

### Risks of pursuing it too early
- turns a static editorial site into a full product platform
- introduces account systems, permissions, privacy, sync, and support overhead
- risks shifting attention away from the core publication before it is truly excellent
- depends heavily on third-party APIs and access patterns that may be unstable or limited
- increases the security and data-handling burden significantly

In other words:
this is a strong second-order opportunity, not the first hill to die on.

---

## The Better Sequence
### Stage 1
Prove Screen Share as a strong editorial product covering movies and TV.

### Stage 2
Build the internal editorial engine that can assemble coherent issues from structured inputs.

### Stage 3
Test limited personalisation manually or semi-manually using a single user profile.

### Stage 4
Explore whether that engine can become a product platform for personalised Screen Share editions.

This sequence matters because it lets the platform emerge from a working editorial engine rather than a speculative app concept.

---

## Product Principle
The future opportunity is strongest if Screen Share becomes:

**not a recommendation engine**

but

**an editorial intelligence layer over a person's watching life**

That means the value is not simply:
- better recommendations
- more data about viewing

The value is:
- interpretation
- taste reflection
- cultural context
- mood and habit awareness
- a more intelligent relationship to what someone watches

---

## Critical Dependencies
If this direction is explored later, key questions will include:

- Which sources are actually accessible and stable?
- What TV and movie viewing signals are most reliable?
- How much can Trakt carry versus other services?
- What is realistically possible with Letterboxd?
- What privacy and consent model is acceptable?
- How much of the experience can remain low-friction and low-maintenance?

These questions should be explored only after the core publication proves itself.

---

## Current Recommendation
Keep this as a deliberate future direction, not a present pivot.

Design the current system so that:
- Screen Share works beautifully as a publication
- its content pipeline becomes modular
- its editorial engine can eventually support personalised inputs
- both movies and TV remain first-class citizens in the model

That way, the future platform remains possible without hijacking the present build.

---

## In One Line
Screen Share should first become an excellent editorial publication about movies, TV, and the culture of watching; only then should it be explored as a personalised platform that generates Screen Share editions from an individual's viewing life.
