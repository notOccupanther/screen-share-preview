Truth.

The camera was never just a witness. It was always an argument. The Thin Blue Line, JFK, Ripley, Adolescence — and twenty-one more films about the gap between what happened and what we’re told.

This week — The Thin Blue Line (1988)

Editor’s Note

There’s a strange comfort in learning that cinema has been worried about this longer than we have. The Thin Blue Line came out in 1988 — a film built from re-enactments, contradictions, and a Philip Glass score that made the whole thing feel like a fever dream of justice. Errol Morris didn’t point a camera at the truth and press record.

He made a film that functioned like a legal argument: methodical, obsessive, built on the premise that if you look at something long enough and from enough angles, something true will eventually surface. It got Randall Adams released from prison. The camera as instrument of justice rather than witness to it. That felt important to sit with, particularly right now, when the shows I’ve been watching — Ripley, Adolescence, Apple Cider Vinegar — are all fundamentally about the gap between performance and fact. How long someone can maintain a story. How much we want to believe it. These aren’t just thriller mechanics. They’re the question the whole issue is circling.

What does it take to actually know something?

The Rewind | From the archive — why this film, why now
One film from the archive that speaks to right now. Not nostalgia — relevance. The film is chosen for what it still has to say about the week’s theme, not for sentiment.
The Thin Blue Line
1988 — Dir. Errol Morris — MUBI / Apple TV+

In 1976, a Dallas police officer was shot dead during a routine traffic stop. A drifter named Randall Adams was convicted and sentenced to death. He was almost certainly innocent. Errol Morris spent two years pulling the case apart, and then did something no documentary filmmaker had done before: he put it back together — not as it happened, but as it might have happened, over and over, from different angles, under different light. The Thin Blue Line didn’t just expose a wrongful conviction. It got Adams released. And it did so by constructing fictions.

That paradox is where the film lives, and it’s never fully resolved — which is why, almost forty years later, it still refuses to sit still.

What Morris Did That No One Else Would

Documentaries, by 1988, had a settled grammar: talking heads, archival footage, narration that held the viewer’s hand toward a conclusion. Morris threw that out. The Thin Blue Line uses no narrator. It lets its subjects contradict each other without editorial intervention. And it reconstructs the night of the murder repeatedly — the same milkshake cup, the same stretch of road, the same gunshot — each time slightly differently, each time implicating a different version of events.

Philip Glass’s score makes this stranger still. It doesn’t soothe or editorialize. It accumulates, cycling through the same melodic fragments like a machine that can’t stop asking the same question. The effect is hypnotic in a way that feels almost coercive — you’re being pulled somewhere, but the destination keeps shifting.

Why This Matters Now, in Particular

We are, in 2026, some years into a genuine epistemological crisis. Footage of events that didn’t happen is generated in seconds. Voices are cloned. The dominant anxiety is that we can no longer trust what we see. Morris anticipated this — not the technology, but the underlying problem — in 1988. The Thin Blue Line argued that there is no neutral footage, no unmediated image, no reconstruction that doesn’t carry the fingerprints of the person doing the reconstructing. The camera was never just a witness. It was always an argument.

Here’s the thing that should make you uncomfortable: the film worked. Randall Adams walked free, in part because of what Morris built. And what Morris built was a series of staged images, lit and scored for maximum effect, designed to lead you to a conclusion. Does that make it truth or manipulation? The honest answer is both. The Thin Blue Line is a film that got an innocent man freed by being, in certain technical senses, dishonest about its own nature. If that makes you uneasy, it should. It should also make you think about every documentary you’ve ever trusted.

Watch Alongside
Standard Operating Procedure (2008, dir. Errol Morris)
Morris turns the same forensic lens on the Abu Ghraib photographs — images the world thought it understood — and dismantles that certainty completely. Where The Thin Blue Line asks whether a reconstruction can reveal truth, Standard Operating Procedure asks whether real photographs can conceal it. Together, they form the sharpest argument in cinema about the limits of the image as evidence.
The Argument | Two readings, one question, no easy answer
Every issue we pick one interpretive question and lay out two genuine positions. Not a straw man and a rebuttal — two real arguments you might actually hold. Vote and see where readers land.
“Documentaries manipulate. Fiction illuminates. We’ve always known this — and ignored it. Now that AI can fabricate footage indistinguishable from the real thing, does the lie finally matter?”
Reading A
The lie always mattered — we chose complicity

The contract between documentary and audience has never been what we pretended it was. When Errol Morris reconstructed crime scenes for The Thin Blue Line, complete with dramatised gunshots and slow-motion milkshakes, audiences accepted it as investigative truth. When Robert Flaherty staged Nanook’s igloo scenes — cutting away walls so the camera could fit, asking his subjects to perform a life they no longer lived — nobody called it fiction. We weren’t deceived. We cooperated.

AI fabricated footage doesn’t introduce manipulation into documentary. It removes the deniability that made manipulation comfortable. The question isn’t whether we’ve been lied to. It’s whether we can keep pretending we didn’t ask for it.

Reading B
AI changes nothing fundamental — truth was always negotiated

Come and See — the most devastating film ever made about the Second World War — features no archival footage, no survivors’ testimony. Shot forty years after the events it depicts. It is, by almost any measure, truer to what happened on the Eastern Front than anything captured at the time. The Battle of Algiers was mistaken by so many audiences for a documentary that the filmmakers added a title card clarifying it wasn’t.

The current panic about AI-generated footage assumes there was once a golden age when documentary meant something pure. There wasn’t. The panic is real, but it’s not about truth — it’s about power: who gets to fabricate, with what resources, in whose interest.

Flashpoint | The moment that still divides
One moment in cinema history that still divides audiences — a scene, a release, or an argument that never quite resolved.
Flashpoint
JFK (1991, dir. Oliver Stone)

Oliver Stone’s JFK arrived in December 1991 with the force of something that wanted to be believed. The film reconstructs the assassination of President Kennedy through the eyes of New Orleans district attorney Jim Garrison, and it does so using every instrument in cinema’s persuasion toolkit: handheld urgency, grainy archival-style inserts, rapid-fire montage, re-enacted footage cut against real documentary material until the two become nearly indistinguishable. Stone invented composite characters who speak to Garrison in parking garages and hotel rooms, delivering exposition as revelation. He staged scenarios he openly acknowledged were speculative, then filmed them with the visual grammar of fact. The centrepiece — a meticulous reconstruction of Dealey Plaza arguing a second shooter on the grassy knoll — was presented with the confidence of a closing argument. When the film grossed over $200 million worldwide and landed eight Academy Award nominations, it was clear Stone hadn’t made a fringe document. He had made a mainstream event. The political fallout was immediate: by 1992, Congress had passed the JFK Records Collection Act, mandating the release of classified government documents. A Hollywood film had moved legislation.

What remains unresolved is the question the film smuggled in under its three-hour runtime: what does a filmmaker owe an audience when the story they’re telling cannot be verified? Stone has been candid that JFK is not a documentary — that dramatic licence was taken. But the film never communicates this to the viewer sitting in the dark. Its style insists otherwise. When cinema borrows the aesthetic vocabulary of journalism — the shaky cam, the found-footage texture, the procedural momentum — it carries an implicit promise. Audiences read these signals as markers of credibility. When that vocabulary is deployed in service of a theory that remains, decades later, contested and unproven, the line between artistic interpretation and manufactured certainty starts to blur. The defenders of JFK argue it opened a conversation that needed opening, that scepticism about the Warren Commission was entirely legitimate, that art has always been allowed to ask destabilising questions in destabilising ways. The critics argue that millions of people left those cinemas not with questions but with answers — and that Stone knew they would. Both things can be true at once. That’s what makes JFK a genuinely difficult film to hold, not because of what it claims, but because of how fluently it claims it.

The Signal | AI in film — three stories this week
Three industry stories every week: a Deal, a Resistance, and a Tool — tracking how AI, money, and power are reshaping what gets made and how.
Deal
Resemble AI Raises $13M to Fight Synthetic Media Fraud
In February 2026, deepfake detection startup Resemble AI closed a $13 million strategic round backed by Sony Innovation Fund, Google’s AI Futures Fund, Comcast Ventures, and Okta Ventures. The funding accelerates global rollout of DETECT-3B Omni, the company’s enterprise detection model claiming 98% accuracy across more than 38 languages. Deepfake-related fraud hit $1.56 billion in losses in 2025. The investor list — spanning hardware, software, and media — signals the industry now treats authentication as infrastructure, not a niche.
Source: Los Angeles Times — More info here
Resistance
Archival Producers Alliance Sets AI Standards for Documentary
At IDFA in November 2025, members of the Archival Producers Alliance published a comprehensive set of Best Practices for the use of generative AI in archive-led documentary filmmaking. The guiding principle is transparency: audiences must know when AI has been used. The International Documentary Association has since hosted sessions on the guidelines. It’s the first formal industry framework to draw a line between AI as a tool and AI as a fabrication.
Source: MIT OpenDocLab — More info here
Tool
Sony Puts a Cryptographic Signature in Your Camera
Sony became the first camera manufacturer to embed C2PA content credentials directly into video files — via firmware updates to the FX3, FX30, a9 III, a1 II, and the professional PXW-Z300 camcorder (October–November 2025). Each clip gets a tamper-evident digital signature at the point of capture, creating a verifiable chain of provenance from camera to edit suite to distribution. For documentary and news cinematographers, it’s the clearest practical answer yet to the question: how do you prove this footage is real?
Source: DPReview — More info here

Project Hail Mary
Phil Lord & Christopher Miller / Amazon MGM, 2026
A man wakes up alone in deep space with no memory of who he is or why he’s there. Lord and Miller’s adaptation of Andy Weir’s novel opens in radical disorientation: Ryland Grace must reconstruct himself from evidence, using the scientific method as a kind of existential detective work. Truth as something pieced together rather than simply known.

Anatomy of a Fall
Justine Triet / Palme d’Or Winner, 2023
In Justine Triet’s Palme d’Or masterwork, a woman is tried for her husband’s death in the French Alps. The courtroom becomes a machine for manufacturing competing truths — audio recordings played, reinterpreted, weaponised. The only eyewitness is their partially-sighted son. Now on Netflix: essential.

Lee Haven Jones’s four-part drama dramatises the story of Jane Andrews, royal dresser to Sarah Ferguson and, in 2001, a convicted murderer. Andrews refused any involvement — making The Lady a reconstruction built entirely from the outside. A case study in whose truth gets told, and how class and proximity to power shapes what survives as fact.

On Screen | Television worth your time
Television worth your time. Airing now, on the horizon, and under the radar — across every platform, curated by what’s actually good.
Airing Now
Apple TV+ · Season 2 · Finale this week
Severance
The show built entirely on the premise that truth is what you’re denied. Season 2 digs deeper into Lumon’s architecture of institutional deception. The severed mind as the perfect metaphor for a culture of managed ignorance.
Netflix · Limited Series · Streaming now
Adolescence
Shot in unbroken single takes, each episode follows a different participant in the investigation of a 13-year-old accused of murder. A forensic portrait of how truth fractures across testimony. No one is lying, exactly. Everyone has a different piece of it.
Netflix · Limited Series · Streaming now
Zero Day
Robert De Niro as a former US president chairing a national inquiry into a catastrophic cyberattack. Truth here is the thing every power player wants to own — and the one thing the inquiry was never really designed to find.
Netflix · Limited Series · Streaming now
Apple Cider Vinegar
Based on the true story of wellness influencer Belle Gibson, who fabricated a cancer diagnosis and built an empire on it. How a constructed narrative becomes belief, belief becomes brand, and brand becomes too lucrative for anyone to question.
Worth Your Time
Paramount+ · Season 1 · Streaming now
The Agency
Michael Fassbender as a CIA operative whose deep-cover identity and real self collide. Espionage as a study in constructed selfhood — identity as operational fiction, intimacy as the one thing you can’t fully surveil. Adapted from French masterwork Le Bureau des Légendes.
Apple TV+ · Season 2 · Streaming now
Silo
Engineer Juliette Nichols goes deeper into the one question the silo is designed to prevent: what is the world outside, and who decided that knowledge was forbidden? Season 2 leans hard into the mechanics of information control.
Apple TV+ · Currently airing
The Studio
Seth Rogen’s Hollywood satire about a studio head navigating the chasm between artistic integrity and commercial survival. How the film industry manufactures conviction, sells manufactured reality, and rewards the confident bluff over the honest doubt.
Paramount+ · Season 1 · Streaming now
Landman
Taylor Sheridan’s oil-industry drama follows a fixer whose job is to make inconvenient truths disappear — quietly, legally, efficiently. The show lives in the gap between corporate narrative and field reality. Truth here is always a liability.
On the Radar
Apple TV+ · Seasons 1–4 · Season 5 in production
Slow Horses
Gary Oldman leads the finest espionage ensemble on television as disgraced MI5 officers handling the cases the Service would rather didn’t exist. Every season unpicks a different lie being maintained by someone with institutional authority and a reason to keep the lid on.
Netflix · Season 1 · Streaming now
Ripley
Andrew Scott as Tom Ripley. Shot in black and white by Steven Zaillian — eight episodes, a man who invents himself from scratch. Identity theft, forgery, and an entire life constructed on the audacity of a sustained lie. The most beautifully made thing on Netflix in the last two years.
Netflix · Limited Series · Streaming now
The Residence
A murder in the White House. Every guest at a state dinner is a suspect. Shonda Rhimes’ gleefully eccentric whodunit earns its place here because its setting is the ultimate stage of constructed public reality — a building where every room has a different official version.
Netflix · Season 2 · Streaming now
The Diplomat
Keri Russell’s accidental ambassador navigates international crisis with competing intelligence briefings and unreliable allies. Season 2 sharpens the show’s central insight: in diplomacy, truth is always the negotiating position you haven’t disclosed yet.
The Continent | Subtitles Required
The best television being made right now is frequently not in English. This section exists because subtitle aversion is costing you some of the sharpest writing, directing, and performance in the medium. No excuses.
Germany · Netflix · 3 Seasons · Complete
Dark
German
The most rigorous argument on television for why truth is not a fixed point but a question of when. Across three seasons, Dark builds a closed causal loop in which every secret unearthed simply relocates the lie — memory corrupts, testimony shifts, and the characters who believe they are finally seeing clearly are always, structurally, the last to know. In a media landscape of easy revelations, Dark insists that uncovering the truth may change nothing at all.
Italy · Sky Atlantic / Netflix · 1 Season · Complete
1994
Italian
Set during the implosion of Italy’s First Republic — Tangentopoli still burning, Berlusconi ascending — 1994 dramatises the moment a nation collectively decided which version of events it could live with, and chose badly. It refuses a single truth-teller, distributing moral compromise so evenly across prosecutors, spin doctors, and TV executives that the viewer is implicated in the same exhausted bargain as the electorate. Watch it and you will understand every political landscape you’ve inhabited since.
Norway · Netflix · 3 Seasons · Complete
Occupied
Norwegian
Jo Nesbø’s scenario — Russia quietly occupying Norway with EU backing after a green-energy pivot — was considered provocative fantasy when it aired in 2015. Considerably less funny now. A slow-burn study in the official lie, the diplomatic non-statement, and the bureaucratic violence of language stripped of meaning. The truth here is visible, filmed, documented — and it changes nothing. That’s the darkest proposition of all.
France · Canal+ / Walter Presents · 8 Seasons · Complete
Spiral
French
Eight seasons and no single character you could call honest, not entirely, not all the way down. Spiral treats the Paris justice system as an ecology of competing truths: the police version, the juge d’instruction’s version, the defence lawyer’s version, each internally coherent and mutually incompatible. It is not cynical about this — it is precise, which is harder. The truth in Spiral exists; it’s just that the machinery built to find it was designed, in part, to lose it.
Discover | Match your mood to a film
Seven moods, three films each. Not best-of lists — mood-matched picks for wherever you are tonight.
When you want your assumptions dismantled
The ground shifts under you, and the version of events you were so sure about simply stops holding.
Rashomon
1950 — Akira Kurosawa
Four irreconcilable accounts of a single violent incident. Not about who’s lying — about the terrifying possibility that everyone is telling the truth they need to tell.
The Act of Killing
2012 — Joshua Oppenheimer
Death squad leaders recreate their own massacres in the genres they love. One of the most genuinely disorienting documents ever made — the perpetrators are gleeful, then confused, then something crumbles.
Stories We Tell
2012 — Sarah Polley
Polley investigates a family secret about her own parentage, then complicates every account by showing you the seams of the film itself. About how families construct usable versions of the past.
When you need to see how the story gets made
You want to sit inside the grind of it — the calls that don’t get returned, the moment the shape of something becomes visible.
Spotlight
2015 — Tom McCarthy
The Boston Globe’s investigation into systemic clergy abuse. Almost procedural in its refusal to dramatise. The horror builds through accumulation. When it lands, it lands hard.
Zodiac
2007 — David Fincher
The Zodiac killings as a film about obsession masquerading as investigation. What happens to people who can’t stop looking even when the looking is slowly destroying them.
Citizenfour
2014 — Laura Poitras
Poitras was already filming when Snowden contacted her. A Hong Kong hotel room. A man methodically dismantling the world’s assumptions about surveillance, unfolding in real time.
When you want beautiful, careful lies
Films that tell the truth sideways, through invented people in invented situations that somehow get closer to real feeling.
A Separation
2011 — Asghar Farhadi
A moral labyrinth from a custody dispute and a wrongful accusation in Tehran. Nobody is simply wrong. Fiction that functions like a philosophy seminar.
The Sweet Hereafter
1997 — Atom Egoyan
A school bus accident and its aftermath through a fractured chronology that mirrors how grief refuses linear time. The truth of a community catastrophe is never the official version.
Certified Copy
2010 — Abbas Kiarostami
A British author and a French dealer through the Italian countryside. Their relationship gradually becomes uncertain. Are they strangers? A long-married couple? Both? A copy authenticated as real becomes real.
When reality feels unreliable
Films that put you inside the paranoia rather than watching it from a safe distance.
The Conversation
1974 — Francis Ford Coppola
A surveillance expert convinced the recording he’s made will be used for a murder. The more he listens, the less certain he is. Quiet, claustrophobic, and finally vertiginous.
Caché
2005 — Michael Haneke
Anonymous surveillance tapes sent to a comfortable Parisian couple. The film is relentless in its refusal to resolve. Who is sending the tapes remains unanswered, deliberately. Watch the final shot twice.
Mulholland Drive
2001 — David Lynch
A dream about Hollywood that collapses in on itself at the midpoint. The first half is intoxicating and the second is like waking from it. One of the films that genuinely reorganises how you watch everything else.
When you want the testimony
Films that make space for a single voice or account — and ask you to sit with what you can and cannot verify.
The Thin Blue Line
1988 — Errol Morris
Reconstructs the 1976 murder of a Dallas police officer and the conviction of a man who almost certainly didn’t do it. The film that invented a genre and got an innocent man released from death row.
The Look of Silence
2014 — Joshua Oppenheimer
An optometrist quietly confronts the men who killed his brother in the 1965 Indonesian massacres while testing their vision. The power is in the restraint. Unbearable, necessary, utterly unlike anything else.
Standard Operating Procedure
2008 — Errol Morris
Morris takes the Abu Ghraib photographs and interviews the people inside them. Asks what the images actually prove — and what happened just outside their frame. The soldiers’ accounts are banal, self-serving, and somehow more troubling than the pictures.
When you need the long view
Films that show how truth gets buried for decades — and what it takes to dig it back up.
Shoah
1985 — Claude Lanzmann
Nine-and-a-half hours. Not a single frame of archival footage — only the present, survivors and perpetrators speaking into the camera. The definitive argument for testimony over archive.
The Battle of Algiers
1966 — Gillo Pontecorvo
Looks so much like newsreel footage that distributors had to disclaim it. Refuses to simplify either side, which is why both French authorities and revolutionary groups screened it as a training document.
Icarus
2017 — Bryan Fogel
Fogel starts a personal doping experiment and accidentally stumbles into the Russian state doping programme. What begins as a cycling doc becomes a whistleblower thriller in real time. The film’s accidental nature gives it an energy no scripted version could manufacture.
When you just want to be unsettled
Finish a film uncertain of what you witnessed — not confused, but genuinely disturbed by how much it left unresolved.
Funny Games
1997 — Michael Haneke
Haneke systematically destroys every convention of the thriller genre, including your right to catharsis. At one point a character picks up the remote and rewinds the film. The version you watch and the one he’s making are not the same film.
The Wailing
2016 — Na Hong-jin
A rural Korean mystery that accumulates dread through sheer tonal instability. You genuinely cannot fix the genre, and that instability is the point. The final sequence is one of the most discussed endings in contemporary world cinema.
Dogtooth
2009 — Yorgos Lanthimos
Parents have raised their adult children in a sealed compound, teaching them a private language where words mean different things. What happens to people whose entire map of reality is a deliberate construction.
