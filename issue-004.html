Theme

Performance
— the role we play, the one they let us

What does it cost to be seen? All About Eve doesn't resolve the question. Neither do we. But the films this week suggest you start by asking who decides what you're allowed to perform.

Lead Film: All About Eve (1950)
Scroll
A note from the editor

Performance is the oldest contract in cinema. You perform; I watch. That contract has always been fragile. The question isn't whether the performance is real — it's who gets to decide what counts as real in the first place. All About Eve understood this in 1950: Eve Harrington doesn't fail because she's fake. She succeeds because her performance is more convincing than Margo's, more complete, more total. The tragedy of the film is that it shows us a world where the best performance wins, and we all know how that ends.

Seventy-five years later, we live in that world. Every screen is a stage. Every scroll is a performance. Every like is a vote on whether your performance was convincing enough. The machinery has gotten faster, the feedback more immediate, the stakes somehow both higher and lower. You perform your life in real time and the audience decides who you are before you finish the sentence.

This issue circles the question that All About Eve raised but never quite answered: What's left when the performance becomes the only thing that matters?

Niall Editor, Screen Share
▶ The Rewind | A film from the archive that speaks to right now
1950 · Joseph L. Mankiewicz

All About Eve

Eve Harrington arrives at a Broadway stage door with a story: she was at every performance, she has loved Margo Channing since she was a child, she has nothing but devotion. Margo takes her under her wing. By the third act, Eve has stolen her roles, her boyfriend, and her future. What All About Eve understands with surgical precision is that performance and deception are not opposites — they are the same skill, deployed in different rooms. Margo is a star who performs Margo Channing eight times a week. Eve is a woman performing devotion, then humility, then talent, then entitlement. The film asks: where is the line between the character you play and who you become when you play it?

Seventy-five years later, the answer has not changed. It has only become faster. The machinery of visibility — social media, streaming, constant documentation — has collapsed the backstage and the stage into one. There is no longer a private self to perform from. You are what you perform, and performance is what survives you. All About Eve captured this moment in 1950 because it understood that performance was never about honesty. It was about power: who gets to decide what you're allowed to be, and for how long you're allowed to be it.

Watch Alongside
Sunset Boulevard (1950, Billy Wilder)
Released the same year, Sunset Boulevard is All About Eve from the other side of the mirror. Norma Desmond is not ascending; she is clinging to a performance that the industry has already written off. Both films understand that in Hollywood, performance is the only currency that matters — and when it's spent, you disappear entirely.

💬 The Argument | One question. No verdict.
“Is there a self beneath the performance, or is the performance all there ever was?”

The streaming age has made performance visible in ways cinema alone never could. You can now watch someone perform their life in real-time, edit it into narrative, and monetise the edit. The person and the persona have become one product. What happens to the question of authenticity when there is no longer a backstage to retreat to?

Reading A — “The Performance Is the Prison”

There is a self underneath, and the performance suffocates it. Watch someone stream for twelve hours and you see a performance of energy that no human body can sustain — because it isn't sustainable. It's a construction. The person inside experiences this as splitting: the version they broadcast and the version that goes home. SAG-AFTRA's central demand in their 2026 negotiations wasn't about money. It was about the right to not be cloned, scanned, and deployed without consent — about the right to a self that isn't monetised. The performance is increasingly the only version of you that survives in public space, and that asymmetry creates a kind of erasure. You become the performance because there's nothing left of you but the data.

Reading B — “The Self Was Always a Performance”

The notion that there's a "true self" waiting behind the performance is the lie we tell ourselves to feel stable. Erving Goffman argued this in 1959: the self is not a thing, it's a repertoire of behaviours adjusted to context. What's changed is not the nature of performance — it's the archive. Every performance used to disappear. Now every performance is permanent, searchable, and separable from context. The crisis isn't that people are "fake." It's that we can now see all our selves at once and experience the contradiction as fragmentation. But the fragmentation was always there. We just had the luxury of not seeing it.

Provocation: If you stream your entire day, and your audience sees the livestream as the real version of you, what exactly have you lied about?

The Argument
All About Eve is, among other things, a film about what it costs to be seen. Margo Channing has paid that cost in full — talent, age, exposure, the whole account. Eve Harrington has found a different arrangement: she lets others do the paying. The question the film won't resolve, and that we're not going to resolve either, is which of them understands performance more honestly. Is the great performer the one who uses the role to finally say something true about themselves? Or is performance always a transaction — the self offered up, renegotiated, gradually replaced? Both readings are available in this film. Neither is comfortable. Pick the one that makes you feel slightly implicated, and start there.
Rewind
1950

All About Eve

There is a moment near the end of All About Eve when Eve Harrington, having taken everything she came for, is herself taken from behind. A teenager named Phoebe has slipped into her apartment. The camera finds Phoebe alone, holding Eve's award, admiring herself in the mirror. Mankiewicz holds the shot just long enough to make the point unanswerable: the performance does not end. It recruits.
Joseph L. Mankiewicz made this film in 1950, and it has not aged because it does not depend on its period. It depends on a structure — the way that performance, once begun, consumes the performer — that is not theatrical nostalgia. It is a working description of what any industry asks of the people it might be willing to use.
Bette Davis's Margo Channing understands this. She is not naïve; she has always known the terms. What she did not anticipate was that the terms would be renegotiated by someone better at performing youth, devotion, and need than she is. Eve does not take Margo's career by force. She takes it by being more convincing.
The film asks: at what point does the performance become the only thing? Margo can answer. Eve cannot. Phoebe will not need to.
In a week when Valerie Cherish is back on screen performing her way through another industry that barely wants her, and Shah Latif — Riz Ahmed's character in Bait, a British-Pakistani actor who deliberately gets himself photographed leaving a Bond audition — is auditioning for a role the country is debating whether to let him have, this film is not archival. It is diagnostic.

Why they belong together

These two shows are the thesis statement of this issue, and they should be read as a diptych. In The Comeback S3, Valerie Cherish — Lisa Kudrow's greatest creation — has signed onto a network sitcom with an AI-written script, executive produced herself, and is documenting the whole thing on camera again. Variety's read is correct: in 2026, everyone is Valerie Cherish. She was always performing relevance; now the machinery of the industry has caught up to her position. Kudrow makes her the sanest adult in a room full of people doing exactly what she has always done, except they can't see it. The show is about the indignity of audition culture absorbed so completely that it becomes personality.

Bait cuts the same argument from the other side of the class-and-race divide. Riz Ahmed plays Shah Latif, a British-Pakistani actor shortlisted to audition for James Bond. The show is a meta-comedy — Shah is auditioning to play a man who is always performing — and Ahmed has written himself a vehicle that lets him show what a brown actor carries into the room that no one in the room will acknowledge. The AV Club's read is the cleanest: Bait isn't about behind-the-scenes industry machinations (unlike The Studio, The Franchise, even The Comeback). It's about what representation costs the person carrying it. Roger Ebert's site calls it "an engaging mix of Uncut Gems and Fleabag." Variety found it "too high-concept" — that dissent is worth flagging, but 18 critics giving it 85 on Metacritic says the majority disagrees.

Together: Valerie Cherish performs for a camera that has now become an AI. Shah Latif auditions for a franchise that was never designed to contain him. Both shows understand that the most exhausting audition is the one you run for yourself, every day.

2020–present

Industry, Season 4

Industry has always been a show about people performing competence they haven't yet earned — graduate recruits at a London investment bank running the character of someone who belongs. Season 4 adds Charlie Heaton and Jack Farthing as new entrants, and the show's engine — Harper Stern's self-invention, Robert Spearing's studied ruthlessness — remains the most sustained examination on television of what professional performance does to actual selfhood. The finance world is the ideal setting: every relationship is also a transaction, every sentence is also a pitch. Season 4 maintains the show's trajectory upward. This is the show that got better as it got more confident about what it was.
Critical temperature: Warm to hot. 87 Metacritic is the best score in the show's run.
2026

How to Get to Heaven from Belfast

Lisa McGee follows Derry Girls with a murder mystery set in 1990s Belfast, where young women have to perform innocence, decorum, and class aspiration within a society that polices all three. The Guardian's five-star review called it essential viewing. The show is about women who have mastered the role they were given and are now using that mastery as cover.
Critical temperature: Extremely warm in UK press; US coverage catching up. 95% RT with 39 reviews is not a fluke.

The Night Manager, Season 2

Jonathan Pine is the character as construct — a man who performs whatever role the spy world requires, who has been performing so long the original self is archaeologically inaccessible. Season 2 puts him back in the field a decade later. Hiddleston is "reliably good" — precisely calibrated. Waiting for the Out

The show is about who gets to claim an identity, and what happens when the people you are performing for can see through it.

Behind the Curtain

The WGA's AI Provisions Are Being Tested in Practice — and the Gaps Are Showing

The 2023 WGA strike won language requiring that AI cannot be credited as a writer and cannot be used to undermine minimums — but enforcement relies on writers knowing when AI has been used in their material, which studios are not always transparent about. The SAG-AFTRA deal similarly restricted AI likeness use but left significant grey areas around "digital doubles" and background performer likenesses. In early 2026, several disputes have emerged between writers and production companies over whether AI-assisted development drafts constitute a breach of MBA minimums. The institutional story is not that the unions lost — they won more than many expected — but that the fight moved from the picket line into contract interpretation, which is slower, less visible, and harder to organise around.

The more precise story is about enforcement infrastructure — the WGA won the rule but hasn't yet built the audit mechanism. That gap is where institutional power reasserts itself quietly.

Colour-Conscious Casting in Prestige TV Has Stalled — and the Discourse Has Moved Backwards

After 2020's visibility peak, data on Black, Latino, and Asian leads in prestige drama has plateaued or declined at several major streamers. DEI programme rollbacks at Disney, Warner Bros. Discovery, and others in late 2024 and early 2025 were framed as budget decisions, but they eliminated the dedicated executives and tracking infrastructure that made inclusion commitments legible and enforceable. Without that infrastructure, casting decisions revert to informal networks — which historically reproduce existing hierarchies. The relevant question for this issue is not diversity-as-representation but diversity-as-labour: who gets to perform which roles, in which rooms, under what conditions.

The more durable angle is institutional: what accountability infrastructure existed, what replaced it, and what that means for who gets cast in what.

The Audition Is Becoming a Screen Test — and the Screen Test May Train the Model

Several casting platforms have introduced AI-assisted audition tools that record, transcribe, and score self-tape submissions. The terms of service for at least two major platforms (Casting Networks, Actors Access) permit the use of submitted footage for system training — though whether this is happening in practice is not publicly confirmed. The structural question raised is real regardless: actors' unpaid audition performances have always been labour without compensation. If that performance now also generates data used to evaluate future performers, it becomes a resource extracted before any contractual relationship exists — meaning the consent and compensation frameworks the unions built do not yet apply at this stage.

This is a question being raised by the architecture of these platforms. The terms permit it; whether it happens is unconfirmed. That distinction matters.

Week of April 11, 2026

THE DEAL

WGA Reaches Four-Year Deal With Studios; AI Training Payment Left on the Table

The Writers Guild of America reached a tentative four-year agreement with the AMPTP on April 4, 2026, after less than a month of negotiations — a notably swift conclusion given the 148-day strike of 2023. The deal includes $321 million in health plan funding, improved streaming residuals, and a requirement that studios notify the WGA if they license writers' work for AI training. However, the studios did not agree to pay writers for that AI training use, which had been a union priority.
Source: Variety, Los Angeles Times, NPR (April 4–9, 2026)
Why this matters: The notification-without-payment provision sets the template for how studios plan to treat writers' intellectual labour as AI feedstock — acknowledged, but not compensated.
THE RESISTANCE

ACTRA Issues "Do Not Work" Notice Over AI Startup Velvet's Casting Practices

On March 19, 2026, the Union of British Columbia Performers (UBCP/ACTRA) issued a Do Not Work notice against Velvet, a US-based AI startup that had used Canadian casting networks to solicit actors for a project it described as an "AI casual conversation" training exercise. The union said Velvet was unable to confirm whether the project would adhere to ACTRA's collective agreement governing AI training use and digital replication of performers.
Source: The Globe and Mail, UBCP/ACTRA official notice (March 19–20, 2026)
Why this matters: This is a direct test of whether AI companies can use the casting pipeline itself — the entry point for performers — as a data-harvesting channel without union oversight.
Note: Story broke March 19; included as prior-week context for direct relevance to the "Performance" theme.
THE TOOL

Bollywood's Collective Artists Network Deploys AI Production Pipeline With Microsoft

Collective Artists Network, one of Bollywood's largest talent agencies, is producing AI-generated video content at its Bengaluru studio using motion-capture data fed into AI character-generation systems. Microsoft confirmed to Reuters that it is providing AI computing infrastructure to Collective to help "shape the next wave of global storytelling." The partnership represents a talent agency — not a traditional studio — taking direct control of AI-generated content production.
Source: Reuters (April 4, 2026)
Why this matters: When talent agencies become AI content producers, the line between representing performers and replacing them with synthetic versions of them collapses.

Week of April 18, 2026

THE DEAL

Paramount Skydance Secures ~$24 Billion From Gulf Sovereign Wealth Funds for Warner Bros. Discovery Takeover

Paramount Skydance locked in equity commitments of nearly $24 billion from three Middle Eastern sovereign wealth funds — led by Saudi Arabia, with participation from Qatar and Abu Dhabi — to back its pending acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery. A WBD shareholder vote on the merger is scheduled for April 23. Paramount has simultaneously restructured its debt financing and expanded a revolving credit facility to $5 billion to prepare for the close.
Source: Variety, Reuters (April 5–10, 2026)
Why this matters: If approved April 23, this deal concentrates ownership of Warner, HBO, DC, CNN, and Max under a single entity partly capitalised by Gulf state funds — reorienting who ultimately decides what stories get told at scale.
THE RESISTANCE

SAG-AFTRA Returns to the Table April 27 With AI Likeness Rights as the Central Fight

SAG-AFTRA and the AMPTP will resume formal contract negotiations on April 27, 2026, under a mutual media blackout, after pausing to allow the WGA's faster-than-expected deal to conclude. National Executive Director Duncan Crabtree-Ireland is leading for the union; AMPTP President Greg Hessinger for the studios. The unresolved core issues are AI guardrails — specifically digital replication and likeness rights for performers — and streaming residuals.
Source: Variety, Deadline, Screen Daily (April 6–7, 2026)
Why this matters: Unlike the WGA fight over written work, SAG-AFTRA's central demand is about ownership of a performer's physical self — what studios can scan, clone, and deploy without the actor present.
THE TOOL

Hollywood Assistants Are Routinely Using AI for Script Coverage and Development, Despite WGA Prohibitions

A Hollywood Reporter investigation published April 8, 2026, found that studio and production company assistants — below the WGA threshold — are regularly using generative AI for first-pass script coverage, notes, and development tasks, often at their employers' tacit encouragement. One assistant quoted: "When they say, 'You should be using AI,' the first thought in your head is: 'Are you asking me to teach you how to replace me with technology?'" The practice routes creative work through AI systems ungoverned by the new WGA protections, which apply only to credited writers.
Source: The Hollywood Reporter (April 8, 2026)
Why this matters: The assistant class — historically the development pipeline's memory and judgment — is being conscripted into AI normalisation at the precise moment their seniors negotiated protections that don't extend downward.
