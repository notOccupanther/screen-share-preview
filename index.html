🎞️ The Rewind | A film from the archive that speaks to right now
1975 · Sydney Pollack

Three Days of the Condor

Robert Redford plays a CIA reader — a man whose entire job is to consume information and notice patterns — who returns from lunch one afternoon to find everyone he works with shot dead. What follows is a film about institutions that murder to protect themselves, and the impossible choice between exposing the truth and surviving it.

There is a particular exhaustion in this moment: the exhaustion of people who still, despite everything, expected better from the systems built to serve them. Pollack made this film in the long shadow of Watergate. It feels, right now, like he made it last Tuesday.

💬 The Argument | One question. No verdict. You decide.
“Is Hereditary a grief film disguised as horror, or horror disguised as grief — and does the answer change how you experience it?”
Reading: Grief First

The supernatural in Hereditary is grief given physical form — the way loss warps a family until they are strangers to each other, speaking different languages in the same house. Paimon is just the name Annie gives to the thing that has lived inside her since childhood: the inherited wound, the mother who never loved her right, the damage passed down not by choice but by blood. The horror is real. But it's the kind that happens in kitchens and bedrooms and at gravesides, and it doesn't need a demon to justify it.

Reading: Horror First

Aster constructs an occult conspiracy from the opening frame — the miniature diorama that zooms into the real world, Joan's face at the support group, the cult that was always already there. The grief is the trap, not the point. Read it as a grief film and you'll feel for Annie. Read it as horror and you'll understand she never had a choice, that every decision she thought she made was already made for her — which is, arguably, the far more terrifying film. Sympathy and dread are not the same emotion.

🎬 Behind the Frame | The craft, up close

Why the final ten minutes of
There Will Be Blood work — and why most films can't do what PTA did

The bowling alley scene works because Paul Thomas Anderson earns his violence the way a novelist earns a death — through two and a half hours of accumulated pressure. Daniel Plainview has not snapped; he has arrived. Every sermon Eli Sunday ever delivered, every handshake Daniel gave while meaning the opposite, every barrel of oil pulled from beneath someone else's land: it all compounds into that single wooden pin swung in a room that used to mean something to both of them. Anderson understood that great cinema doesn't build to a climax. It builds to a revelation. The horror is not that we are shocked. The horror is that we are not. We have been waiting — without knowing we were waiting — and that is what the best films do to you.
